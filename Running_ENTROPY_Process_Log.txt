UNDERSTANDING WAIC
- Running an MCMC means cycling through different iterations of my parameters; different admixture proportions (q) for each individual, different allele frequencies, different population assignments (z) etc. I cycled through 60,000 iterations. 20,000 of them were discarded, because they were likely bigger, less accurate jumps from the "true" parameters of the model. Then I thinned the remaining to just 2,000 iterations.
I gave ENTROPY:
* Genotype calls for each individual at each SNP (i.e. if it's homozygous, or hetero reference/alternate)
* Which alleles exist (i.e. variant calls)
* My number of individuals and variants

ENTROPY then tries to estimate:
* What the allele frequencies are in each population, depending on how many populations I'm telling it there are (k=2,3,4 etc.)
* Admixture proportions (q) for each individual: what fraction of its genome comes from each population?
* Population assignments for each allele: which population did each allele come from?

WHAT WAIC IS DOING (This is an understanding from Seqera, so maybe double check)
For each iteration, i.e. each combination of parameters, like "For a model with these parameters, if we look at SNP X in Individual Y, how likely is it that they would have that genotype if the true population matched these parameters?"

*likdata is storing that information; the likelihood of the genotype of that SNP in that individual, for the current set of model parameters. 

THEN WAIC: looks at the likelihood values across all 2000 iterations. So, let's say SNP #12,000 in individual 50, it looks at the likelihood estimates across all iterations
* if there is a high amount of variation, it means there is more uncertainty
* if there is low variation, it means less uncertainty and more confidence
For all combinations of individuals and SNPs, if there is overall a high amount of uncertainty, it means that the model doesn't fit very well for that value of K 

WHAT DIC DOES
Essentially, looks at an overall deviance value for the model. i.e.
*How well do the parameters in iteration 1 explain the data? How well do the parameters in iteration 2 explain the data?...etc., for every iteration
* Then, it averages them to create an overall measure of deviance/uncertainty for the model for each value of k


It seems like the main difference is that WAIC takes into account and rewards/penalizes uncertainty at the level of individual-SNP combinations. 

A metaphor Seqera provided:
DIC is like saying "We had 100 different students (iterations) take Test A, B, and C (different k values), and overall they got these average grade for each different test. Therefore, test B is the one we should use 

WAIC is like saying: "We had 100 different students (iterations) take Test A, B, and C. For Test A, they did pretty good on most of the questions, but really terribly at some of the questions, for test B they did badly on most of the questions, but they did really well on some questions, for test C they did well across almost all of the questions, therefore test C is the best."

i.e. it takes into account the performance of specific combinations of individual and SNPs

Re-summarized by Seqera with a bit of added context:

DIC approach:
*"What's the overall average grade?"
*Looks at total performance across all questions combined
*Misses the details of which specific questions were hard/easy

WAIC approach:
*"How did they do on each individual question?"
*Recognizes that some questions are consistently easy (low uncertainty)
*Other questions are consistently hard (high uncertainty)
*Penalizes models more for being uncertain about the "hard" questions

Applied to your grass genetics:

DIC says:
*"k=2 model: Students averaged 75% overall"
*"k=3 model: Students averaged 85% overall"
*"k=4 model: Students averaged 82% overall"
*→ Choose k=3

WAIC says:
*"k=2 model: Students did well on easy SNPs but terribly on hybrid-diagnostic SNPs"
*"k=3 model: Students did well on most SNPs, including the tricky hybrid ones"
*"k=4 model: Students did well overall but were inconsistent on some SNPs"
*→ Choose k=3, and we're more confident because it handles the hard cases well

Why this matters for hybrid detection:

Some SNPs are "easy questions":
*Clear population markers
*Any reasonable k value handles them well
*Low uncertainty across iterations

Some SNPs are "hard questions":
*Hybrid-diagnostic SNPs - the ones that distinguish pure species from hybrids
*k=2 struggles with these (forces hybrids into parent categories)
*k=3 handles these well (recognizes hybrids as distinct)
*WAIC gives extra credit for getting these "hard questions" right
